The BlackRock Bitcoin Sell Controversy: What the Telegram Leak Actually Said — and What On-Chain Data Shows

BY EDITORIAL DESK · 7 MIN READ · APRIL 14, 2025
SHARE

A single Telegram post claiming BlackRock had sold $136.28 million worth of Bitcoin caused a brief but sharp panic across crypto markets on April 14. The post circulated in several high-follower channels before being amplified by accounts with combined audiences exceeding 2.4 million. Within 20 minutes, Bitcoin had dropped $1,800 from its session high. The only problem: BlackRock had not confirmed it, and the on-chain evidence told a more complicated story.

KEY FINDINGS — EVIDENCE LEVEL: MULTI-SOURCE
$136.28M Bitcoin sell figure cited in the Telegram post
$1,800 Bitcoin price decline in 20 minutes following initial leak
2.4M Combined follower count of accounts that amplified the post
0 Official statements from BlackRock confirming the sale

What the Post Said — and How It Spread

The original Telegram message appeared at 2:17 PM UTC in a channel named Crypto Insider Alerts, which has approximately 340,000 members. The post included what appeared to be a screenshot of a Bloomberg Terminal entry showing a $136.28 million outflow from BlackRock's IBIT ETF. No timestamp, no context, no attribution to any person or desk. Within four minutes, it had been screenshot and reposted in at least three other channels. By the time it reached Twitter via two accounts each with over 500,000 followers, it had been stripped of the "allegedly" qualifier that the original post had included in fine print.

The market moved before any journalist or institutional desk could verify the claim. This is the new reality of crypto information markets: the rumor cycle is faster than the fact-checking cycle. Algo traders monitoring social sentiment metrics picked up the keyword cluster — BlackRock, sold, $136M, Bitcoin — and triggered programmatic selling before a human had read the post in full. The speed of the reaction says less about the credibility of the source and more about the fragility of a market where unverified Telegram posts can move $1,800 in 20 minutes.

What the On-Chain Data Actually Showed

Blockchain analytics firm Arkham Intelligence pulled the IBIT on-chain wallet activity for the 48 hours surrounding the alleged sale. Their analysis showed net inflows to BlackRock's identified custodial addresses of approximately $47 million during the relevant period — not outflows. The wallet cluster associated with Coinbase Custody, which holds the ETF's underlying Bitcoin, showed no significant movement consistent with a $136 million liquidation. On-chain settlements of that size leave distinctive footprints, and none were found.

The ETF flow data published by Bloomberg Intelligence the following morning showed IBIT with $89 million in net inflows on April 14 — the opposite of what the Telegram post claimed. A BlackRock spokesperson declined to comment specifically on the Telegram post but noted that all material portfolio changes are reported through standard regulatory filings. The post has since been deleted from its originating channel, but not before it had already delivered its market impact. No account was held responsible, and no regulator has opened an inquiry into the incident.

The Deeper Problem This Exposes

This incident is not isolated. In the twelve months prior, at least seven similar Telegram-sourced rumors about institutional Bitcoin selling had moved markets temporarily before being debunked. What distinguishes the BlackRock incident is the specificity — the exact dollar figure, the Bloomberg Terminal screenshot aesthetic — which gave it enough surface plausibility to survive the first wave of skepticism. Sophisticated retail investors who would have dismissed a vaguer claim paused long enough for the momentum to build.

The structural issue is that crypto markets have no equivalent of FINRA's rules on spreading false information for the purpose of affecting securities prices. Decentralized, pseudonymous communication channels are legally difficult to police. Until platforms like Telegram take accountability for financially impactful misinformation at scale, or until market participants collectively develop faster verification reflexes, these incidents will recur. The next one may not be self-correcting within the same trading session.
